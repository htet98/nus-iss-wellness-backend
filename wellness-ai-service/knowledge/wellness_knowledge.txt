# SLEEP
# ─────

Adults need 7–9 hours of sleep per night for optimal health. Consistent sleep and wake times — even on weekends — regulate the circadian rhythm and improve sleep quality. Avoid screens 1 hour before bed because blue light suppresses melatonin. A cool room (15–18 °C) promotes deeper sleep. Sleep deprivation impairs memory, mood, and immune function.

Sleep stages: NREM stages 1–3 handle physical repair; REM sleep consolidates memory and emotional processing. Both are essential. A full sleep cycle is approximately 90 minutes; aim for 5 complete cycles (≈7.5 hours) per night.

Napping: A 20-minute nap before 3 pm boosts alertness without disrupting night sleep. Naps longer than 30 minutes cause grogginess (sleep inertia). A "caffeine nap" — drinking coffee immediately before a 20-minute nap — combines caffeine's delayed kick with the nap's immediate benefit.

Insomnia strategies: cognitive behavioural therapy for insomnia (CBT-I) is more effective than sleep medication long-term and has no side-effects. Core techniques include sleep restriction therapy, stimulus control, and sleep hygiene. Progressive muscle relaxation and deep breathing are useful add-ons.

Sleep hygiene checklist:
- Keep the bedroom dark and quiet.
- Reserve the bed only for sleep and sex — not working or watching TV.
- Avoid caffeine after 2 pm (caffeine half-life is 5–6 hours).
- Limit alcohol — it fragments sleep in the second half of the night.
- Exercise regularly, but not within 2 hours of bedtime for some people.

Melatonin supplements (0.5–3 mg) can help shift sleep timing (e.g. jet lag) but are not a strong sedative. They work best taken 30–60 minutes before your desired bedtime.


# EXERCISE
# ────────

The WHO recommends at least 150 minutes of moderate-intensity aerobic activity or 75 minutes of vigorous activity per week, plus muscle-strengthening exercises on 2 or more days per week.

Types of exercise:
- Aerobic (cardio): running, cycling, swimming, brisk walking — improves heart health, burns calories, boosts mood via endorphins and serotonin.
- Strength training: resistance exercises (weights, resistance bands, bodyweight) preserve and build muscle mass, increase resting metabolism, support bone density, and improve insulin sensitivity.
- Flexibility: yoga, static stretching — reduces injury risk, improves posture and range of motion. Hold static stretches 20–30 seconds after exercise, not before.
- Balance training: important for older adults to prevent falls. Single-leg exercises, Tai Chi.
- HIIT (High-Intensity Interval Training): short bursts at near-maximal effort followed by rest. Efficient for cardiovascular fitness and fat loss in less time. E.g. 20s sprint / 40s rest × 10 rounds.

Progressive overload: gradually increasing weight, reps, sets, or training density over time is essential for continued strength and muscle gains. The body adapts to the same stimulus within 4–6 weeks.

Running improvement:
- Interval training 1–2× per week builds speed (e.g. 6 × 400 m at 5 km race pace with 90 s rest).
- Easy aerobic runs 2–3× per week build aerobic base (conversational pace, 60–70% max HR).
- One rest or active recovery day per week prevents overtraining.
- Increase weekly mileage by no more than 10% per week to avoid injury.

Strength training:
- Allow 48 hours recovery between sessions targeting the same muscle group.
- Common splits: upper/lower (4 days), push-pull-legs (6 days), full body (3 days).
- Compound movements (squat, deadlift, bench press, pull-up) recruit the most muscle and stimulate the most growth.
- Aim for 3–5 sets of 6–12 reps at 65–80% of 1-rep max for hypertrophy.

Bodyweight exercises (no equipment needed):
- Push-ups: chest, shoulders, triceps. Elevate feet for more shoulder emphasis.
- Squats and lunges: quads, glutes, hamstrings.
- Pull-ups / inverted rows: back and biceps.
- Plank and dead bug: core stability.
- Burpees: full-body cardio.

Signs of overtraining: persistent fatigue, declining performance, irritability, poor sleep, frequent illness. Remedy: reduce volume by 40–50% for 1–2 weeks.

Exercise and mood: a single 20–30 minute moderate-intensity session raises mood for 1–3 hours. Regular exercise reduces risk of depression and anxiety by 30–40% over the long term.


# NUTRITION
# ─────────

Macronutrients:
- Protein (4 kcal/g): essential for muscle repair and satiety. Target 1.6–2.2 g per kg body weight for those doing resistance training. Sources: lean meat, fish, eggs, dairy, legumes, tofu, tempeh.
- Carbohydrates (4 kcal/g): primary energy source for the brain and muscles. Choose complex carbs (oats, brown rice, sweet potato, quinoa, wholegrain bread) for sustained energy. Limit refined sugars and ultra-processed foods.
- Fats (9 kcal/g): necessary for hormone production, brain health, and fat-soluble vitamins (A, D, E, K). Prioritise unsaturated fats (olive oil, avocado, nuts, fatty fish). Limit saturated fats and avoid trans fats.

Micronutrients commonly deficient:
- Vitamin D: essential for bone health and immunity. Sources: sunlight, fatty fish, fortified milk.
- Iron: important for oxygen transport. Deficiency causes fatigue. Sources: red meat, spinach, lentils. Pair plant iron with vitamin C to improve absorption.
- Magnesium: supports sleep, muscle function, blood sugar. Sources: nuts, seeds, leafy greens, dark chocolate.
- Calcium: bone and teeth health. Sources: dairy, fortified soy milk, leafy greens, sardines.
- Omega-3 fatty acids (EPA/DHA): anti-inflammatory, heart and brain health. Sources: salmon, mackerel, sardines, walnuts, flaxseed.

Weight management:
- A caloric deficit of approximately 500 kcal/day leads to roughly 0.5 kg fat loss per week.
- Crash diets slow metabolism, cause muscle loss, and lead to rebound weight gain.
- Protein is the most satiating macronutrient — higher protein intake helps maintain muscle during a deficit.

Meal timing:
- Pre-workout: a mixed carb + protein meal 2–3 hours before exercise, or a small snack (banana, protein shake) 30–60 minutes before.
- Post-workout: consume protein within 30–60 minutes to maximise muscle protein synthesis. 20–40 g protein is a practical target.
- Intermittent fasting (16:8, 5:2): effective for some people for weight management. No magic beyond caloric restriction; choose the eating pattern you can sustain.

Fibre: aim for 25–38 g/day from vegetables, fruit, legumes, and wholegrains. Fibre feeds beneficial gut bacteria, improves satiety, and reduces risk of colorectal cancer.

Gut health: a diverse diet with 30+ different plant foods per week supports microbiome diversity. Fermented foods (yoghurt, kefir, kimchi, tempeh) add beneficial probiotics. Prebiotics (garlic, onion, oats, banana) feed good bacteria.


# HYDRATION
# ─────────

Adults should aim for 2–3 litres of water per day (total fluid including food), more in hot and humid climates like Singapore, and more during exercise. Dehydration of even 1–2% of body weight impairs cognitive performance and physical endurance. Urine colour is a reliable indicator — pale yellow is ideal; dark yellow or amber means drink more.

Electrolytes (sodium, potassium, magnesium, chloride) are lost in sweat and need replacement after intense or prolonged exercise. Sports drinks or electrolyte tablets are useful during sessions over 60–90 minutes.

Caffeine (coffee, tea) is a mild diuretic but the fluid intake from caffeinated beverages still contributes positively to daily hydration balance in moderate amounts (up to 3–4 cups/day).

Signs of dehydration: thirst, dark urine, headache, fatigue, difficulty concentrating, dizziness.


# WEIGHT MANAGEMENT
# ─────────────────

Body composition (muscle-to-fat ratio) is more important than scale weight alone. Resistance training while in a caloric deficit preserves muscle. Track progress with body measurements, progress photos, and how clothes fit — not just scale weight.

Sustainable fat loss: 0.5–1% of body weight per week. Losing faster typically means losing muscle alongside fat. Very low calorie diets (<1,200 kcal/day) are hard to sustain and often backfire.

BMI (Body Mass Index) is a population-level screening tool, not a perfect individual measure. It does not account for muscle mass or fat distribution. Asian populations have higher metabolic risk at lower BMIs; the Asian cut-off for overweight is BMI ≥ 23 (vs. ≥ 25 globally).

Visceral fat (fat around internal organs) is more harmful than subcutaneous fat. Waist circumference is a better predictor of metabolic risk: >90 cm (men) or >80 cm (women) signals elevated risk for Asian adults.

Factors that affect weight: sleep quality, stress (cortisol drives fat storage), gut microbiome, medication side-effects, thyroid function, hormonal changes.


# MENTAL HEALTH & STRESS
# ──────────────────────

Exercise is one of the most effective interventions for mild-to-moderate depression and anxiety. Even a 20-minute walk raises mood for several hours via endorphin and serotonin release.

Stress management techniques:
- Diaphragmatic breathing: activates the parasympathetic nervous system within minutes. Breathe into the belly, not the chest.
- Box breathing: 4 s inhale → 4 s hold → 4 s exhale → 4 s hold. Repeat 4–6 times.
- 4-7-8 breathing: inhale 4 s, hold 7 s, exhale 8 s. Useful before sleep.
- Progressive muscle relaxation: tense and release each muscle group from toes to head.

Mindfulness and meditation: even 5–10 minutes of daily practice reduces anxiety, improves emotional regulation, and can lower blood pressure over weeks. Mindfulness-Based Stress Reduction (MBSR) is an evidence-based 8-week programme.

Cognitive distortions: common unhelpful thinking patterns include catastrophising, all-or-nothing thinking, mind-reading, and personalisation. Cognitive Behavioural Therapy (CBT) helps identify and reframe these patterns.

Social connection: strong social relationships are one of the most robust predictors of longevity and wellbeing. Prioritise face-to-face interaction. Loneliness has health effects comparable to smoking 15 cigarettes a day.

Journalling: writing 3 things you are grateful for each day has measurable effects on mood within 2 weeks. Expressive writing about stressful events for 15–20 minutes can reduce their emotional impact.

When to seek professional help: persistent sadness or anxiety lasting more than 2 weeks, significant changes in sleep or appetite, loss of interest in activities, difficulty functioning at work or in relationships.


# STEPS & SEDENTARY BEHAVIOUR
# ────────────────────────────

10,000 steps per day is a popular target, but research shows meaningful health benefits begin at approximately 7,000–8,000 steps/day for most adults. Each additional 1,000 steps reduces cardiovascular risk. Walking breaks of 5–10 minutes every hour offset the health risks of prolonged sitting.

Prolonged sitting is an independent risk factor for cardiovascular disease, metabolic syndrome, and poor posture — even if you exercise regularly. Aim to stand or move for at least 2 minutes every 30–60 minutes during desk work.

A brisk 30-minute walk (approximately 3,000–4,000 steps) most days meets WHO aerobic activity recommendations and has significant cardiovascular benefits.


# HEART HEALTH
# ────────────

Key risk factors for cardiovascular disease: high blood pressure, high LDL cholesterol, smoking, diabetes, obesity, physical inactivity, family history, age, and chronic stress.

Normal blood pressure: below 120/80 mmHg. Elevated: 120–129/<80. Stage 1 hypertension: 130–139/80–89. Diet, exercise, and weight loss can reduce blood pressure by 5–10 mmHg without medication.

Diet for heart health: emphasise vegetables, fruit, wholegrains, legumes, nuts, olive oil, and fish (Mediterranean diet). Limit red and processed meat, added sugar, and ultra-processed food. Reduce dietary sodium (aim <2,300 mg/day).

Cholesterol: LDL ("bad") should ideally be below 2.6 mmol/L. HDL ("good") should be above 1.0 mmol/L (men) or 1.2 mmol/L (women). Triglycerides below 1.7 mmol/L.

Regular aerobic exercise lowers blood pressure, raises HDL, reduces triglycerides, and improves arterial flexibility.


# DIABETES PREVENTION & BLOOD SUGAR
# ──────────────────────────────────

Type 2 diabetes is largely preventable: losing 5–10% of body weight if overweight, eating a diet low in refined carbs and sugar, and exercising regularly reduce risk by up to 58%.

Glycaemic index (GI): low-GI foods (oats, sweet potato, legumes, most fruits) cause slower blood sugar rises than high-GI foods (white bread, white rice, sugary drinks). Pairing carbs with protein, fat, or fibre slows glucose absorption.

Signs of high blood sugar: frequent urination, excessive thirst, fatigue, blurred vision. Type 2 diabetes often has no symptoms for years — regular screening is important for Asian adults over 40 or those with family history.

Exercise improves insulin sensitivity: muscle contractions allow glucose uptake without insulin. Even a 10-minute walk after meals reduces post-meal blood sugar spikes.


# SUPPLEMENTS
# ───────────

Most healthy people eating a varied diet do not need supplements. However, some are evidence-based:

- Creatine monohydrate: the most well-researched sports supplement. Increases muscle strength and power output by 5–15%. Safe for long-term use. 3–5 g/day; loading phase is optional.
- Protein powder (whey, casein, plant-based): convenient way to hit daily protein targets. No benefit over food if protein needs are already met.
- Vitamin D (1,000–2,000 IU/day): most people with limited sun exposure benefit.
- Omega-3 fish oil (EPA + DHA, 1–2 g/day): reduces inflammation and triglycerides, supports brain health.
- Magnesium glycinate or malate (200–400 mg/day): may improve sleep quality and reduce muscle cramps.
- Caffeine: 3–6 mg/kg body weight 30–60 minutes before exercise improves endurance performance, alertness, and strength.


# POSTURE & ERGONOMICS
# ────────────────────

Poor posture at a desk causes neck pain, lower back pain, and headaches — common issues for office workers and students.

Ergonomics checklist:
- Monitor top should be at or just below eye level, arm's length away.
- Elbows at 90°, wrists neutral (not bent up or down).
- Hips slightly above knees, feet flat on the floor or a footrest.
- Lower back supported by chair lumbar support or a rolled towel.

Neck and shoulder relief: chin tucks (gently retract head, hold 5 s, repeat 10×) strengthen deep neck flexors and counteract forward head posture.

Lower back: strengthening the core prevents and relieves back pain more effectively than rest. Cat-cow stretch, bird-dog, and dead bug are safe exercises.

Standing desks: alternating between sitting and standing (30 min each) is better than standing all day.


# RECOVERY
# ────────

Active recovery (light movement — walking, yoga, easy cycling) on rest days promotes blood flow and reduces muscle soreness better than complete rest.

Sleep is the most powerful recovery tool. Growth hormone is released primarily during deep NREM sleep; this is when muscle repair occurs.

Nutrition for recovery: consume protein (20–40 g) and carbohydrates within 30–60 minutes after intense exercise. Tart cherry juice (240 ml) has evidence for reducing muscle soreness.

Cold water immersion (10–15 °C, 10–15 minutes) reduces acute muscle soreness and perceived fatigue after intense exercise.

Signs you need more recovery: persistent muscle soreness beyond 72 hours, reduced motivation, declining performance, disrupted sleep, or elevated resting heart rate.


# COMMON WELLNESS MYTHS
# ─────────────────────

Myth: "You need to detox your liver/kidneys with juices or cleanses."
Fact: The liver and kidneys detoxify continuously. No supplement or juice accelerates this.

Myth: "Eating fat makes you fat."
Fact: Excess calories cause fat gain, regardless of source. Dietary fat is essential and does not directly cause weight gain.

Myth: "Muscle turns to fat if you stop training."
Fact: Muscle and fat are different tissues and cannot convert to each other. When training stops, muscle shrinks; fat may increase if caloric intake stays the same.

Myth: "Cardio is the best way to lose weight."
Fact: Diet is the primary driver of fat loss. Strength training is at least as effective as cardio and preserves muscle while losing weight.

Myth: "Stretching before exercise prevents injury."
Fact: Static stretching before exercise can reduce power output. Dynamic warm-up is more effective. Static stretching is best done after exercise.
