# SLEEP

Adults need 7-9 hours of sleep per night for optimal health. Consistent sleep and wake times — even on weekends — regulate the circadian rhythm and improve sleep quality. Avoid screens 1 hour before bed because blue light suppresses melatonin. A cool room (15-18°C) promotes deeper sleep. Sleep deprivation impairs memory, mood, and immune function.

Sleep stages: NREM stages 1-3 handle physical repair; REM sleep consolidates memory and emotional processing. Both are essential. A full sleep cycle is ~90 minutes; aim for 5 complete cycles per night.

Napping: A 20-minute nap before 3pm boosts alertness without disrupting night sleep. Naps longer than 30 minutes cause grogginess (sleep inertia).

Insomnia strategies: cognitive behavioural therapy for insomnia (CBT-I) is more effective than medication long-term. Progressive muscle relaxation and sleep restriction therapy are core CBT-I techniques.

# EXERCISE

The WHO recommends at least 150 minutes of moderate-intensity aerobic activity or 75 minutes of vigorous activity per week, plus muscle-strengthening exercises on 2+ days per week.

Types of exercise:
- Aerobic (cardio): running, cycling, swimming — improves heart health, burns calories, boosts mood via endorphins.
- Strength training: resistance exercises preserve muscle mass, increase metabolism, support bone density.
- Flexibility: yoga, stretching — reduces injury risk and improves range of motion.
- HIIT (High-Intensity Interval Training): short bursts at 90% effort followed by rest. Efficient for fat loss and cardiovascular fitness.

Progressive overload: gradually increase weight, reps, or intensity over time to keep improving. Rest and recovery are as important as training.

Running improvement: interval training 1-2× per week builds speed. Easy aerobic runs 2-3× per week build base fitness. One rest day per week prevents overtraining.

Strength training splits: allow 48 hours recovery between sessions targeting the same muscle group. Upper/lower splits or push/pull/legs are common approaches.

# NUTRITION

Macronutrients:
- Protein (4 kcal/g): essential for muscle repair. Target 1.6-2.2g per kg body weight for athletes. Sources: lean meat, eggs, legumes, tofu.
- Carbohydrates (4 kcal/g): primary energy source. Choose complex carbs (oats, brown rice, sweet potato) for sustained energy.
- Fats (9 kcal/g): necessary for hormone production and fat-soluble vitamins. Prioritise unsaturated fats (olive oil, avocado, nuts).

Weight management: a caloric deficit of ~500 kcal/day leads to ~0.5kg loss per week. Crash diets slow metabolism and cause muscle loss.

Pre-workout: eat a carb+protein meal 2-3 hours before, or a small snack 30-60 minutes before. Post-workout: protein within 30-60 minutes supports muscle repair.

# HYDRATION

Adults should aim for 2-3 litres of water per day, more in hot weather or during exercise. Dehydration of even 1-2% body weight impairs cognitive and physical performance. Urine colour is a reliable indicator — pale yellow is ideal; dark yellow means drink more.

Electrolytes (sodium, potassium, magnesium) are lost in sweat and need replacement after intense exercise. Sports drinks or electrolyte tablets can help during sessions over 60 minutes.

# MOOD & MENTAL WELLNESS

Exercise is one of the most effective interventions for mild-to-moderate depression and anxiety. Even a 20-minute walk raises mood for several hours via endorphin and serotonin release.

Stress management: deep diaphragmatic breathing activates the parasympathetic nervous system and lowers cortisol within minutes. Box breathing (4s in, 4s hold, 4s out, 4s hold) is a practical technique.

Mindfulness and meditation reduce anxiety, improve emotional regulation, and can lower blood pressure. Even 5-10 minutes of daily practice shows measurable benefits.

Sleep and mood: poor sleep amplifies negative emotions and reduces resilience. Improving sleep is often the fastest way to improve mood.

Social connection is a strong predictor of mental wellbeing. Regular meaningful interaction reduces loneliness and supports emotional regulation.

# STEPS

10,000 steps per day is a popular target, but research shows meaningful health benefits begin at ~7,000-8,000 steps/day for most adults. Each 1,000 additional steps reduces cardiovascular risk. Walking breaks of 5-10 minutes every hour offset the health risks of prolonged sitting.

# WEIGHT MANAGEMENT

Body composition (muscle-to-fat ratio) is more important than weight alone. Resistance training while in a caloric deficit preserves muscle. Track progress with measurements and how clothes fit, not just scale weight.

Sustainable fat loss is 0.5-1% of body weight per week. Losing faster typically means losing muscle alongside fat.
